Shocks in the Interstellar Medium


Shocks are a common phenomenon in the ISM. They can be generated by a number of things:

  • Super novae: the explosion expels most of a stars material in a supersonic shockwave that sweeps up surrounding material. Both forward and reverse shocks are present.
  • Novae: smaller explosions can occur on the surfaces on white dwarfs that accrete matter from a binary companion. These also drive shocks into the ISM.
  • Stellar winds: fast stellar winds can create shock fronts as they interact with the ISM.
  • Expanding HII regions: drive shock waves into the surrounding neutral gas.
  • AGN feedback: the supermassive black hole at the center of the galaxy may produce jets or galactic winds that helps drive turbulence and shocks.
  • Supersonic turbulence: feedback from stars (super novae, winds, HII regions) drive supersonic turbulence in the ISM. Clouds in the ISM may collide, driving shock waves.
  • Gas passing through spiral arms: Gas may undergo a shock transition after they are accelerated by gravity and then enter the spiral arms, where the gas is decelerated and compressed.

In short, the ISM is a very dynamic fluid, full of shocks, magnetic fields, and driven supersonic turbulence. In addition, radiative processes play an important role in modifying shock structure (we have not included these effects in our module). Shocked gas is able to cool radiatively if it has propagated long enough time-scales, making energy loss important.
